The Year in Review

It was a year of flux. Several countries installed new leaders in 2025, including Canada, Germany, and the United States, while Japan and Namibia inaugurated their first women heads of government. Generation Z flexed its political might across the globe, toppling national governments in some cases. Meanwhile, in arts and culture, Anora dominated at the Oscars, Beyoncé’s Cowboy Carter won the Grammy Award for best album of the year, and the groundbreaking Bollywood film Sholay turned 50. In science artificial intelligence continued to dominate the conversation. And devastating natural disasters, such as the Myanmar earthquake and floods in South and Southeast Asia, also characterized 2025.

Myanmar’s Election

Yesterday citizens of war-torn Myanmar began voting in a multistage general election, marking the country’s first national vote since 2020; the results of that election were overturned when the military seized power in a 2021 coup.

Civil war

The general election was announced by the ruling military junta, the State Administration Council (SAC), in August, with rounds also scheduled for January 11 and January 25. When the SAC seized control in 2021, it triggered the Myanmar Civil War, which is still raging in parts of the country, with the junta controlling less than half of the country’s territory. Although the SAC presented the current election as a step toward stability, most observers regard it as an attempt to legitimize continued military rule.

Narrow ballot

The National League for Democracy (NLD)—led by detained former state counselor Aung San Suu Kyi—was the principal pro-democracy opposition to military rule. But the NLD was dissolved in 2023 after refusing to register under new party laws it considered undemocratic. Most other opposition groups have likewise been barred, leaving only junta-approved parties eligible to participate. Many regional and international observers question the legitimacy of any election held under such circumstances.

The SAC, internationally

The SAC is largely isolated from the international community, but it has two significant allies in Russia and China. Russia has provided overt military and political support since the 2021 coup, whereas China had initially adopted a more cautious approach, maintaining contacts with both the junta and resistance groups. In late 2025, however, China expanded its patronage of the junta, including military aid, financial assistance, and diplomatic support, seeking to stabilize its border regions and protect its economic interests after years of unrest.
